1The interview & how to use this guide

What the 60-minute OR module actually tests

Per the prep kit, the interviewer evaluates two things: technical depth (can you build a correct, well-structured optimization model live?) and business judgment (do you connect the model to dollars, reliability, and customer experience?). Expect roughly this arc:

  • Past OR projects (~15–20 min). Walk through 2–3 projects: your role, the formulation, the algorithm, and measurable results. Use STAR. Be ready to write the actual objective and constraints of your past models, not just describe them.
  • Novel problem formulation (~30–40 min). A DoorDash-flavored business problem ("How would you decide which orders to batch together?"). You are expected to ask clarifying questions, state assumptions, then formulate — decision variables, objective, constraints — likely as a MIP, on a shared doc or whiteboard.
  • Follow-ups. "What if the problem has 100k variables and must solve in 1 second?" — tests LP relaxation, heuristics, decomposition, and pragmatism.
Interview tipThe single highest-leverage habit: narrate your structure before the math. Say "I'll define the decision, then the objective, then constraints in three groups: capacity, logic, and quality-of-service." Interviewers grade the process as much as the answer.

2Linear programming from the ground up

Anatomy of an optimization problem

Every mathematical program has three ingredients. Getting into the habit of naming them in this order is exactly what the interview expects:

  1. Decision variables — the levers you control. E.g., \(x_{ij}=1\) if Dasher \(i\) is assigned order \(j\).
  2. Objective function — one scalar measure of "good", minimized or maximized. E.g., total delivery time.
  3. Constraints — the rules of the world: capacities, logic, budgets, service guarantees.

A linear program (LP) requires the objective and all constraints to be linear (weighted sums) in continuous variables:

$$\begin{aligned} \min_{x} \quad & c^\top x \\ \text{s.t.} \quad & A x \ge b \\ & x \ge 0 \end{aligned}$$

Here \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\) are decisions, \(c\) the cost vector, each row of \(Ax \ge b\) one constraint. Any mix of \(\le,\ \ge,\ =\) constraints and max/min objectives can be converted into this standard form: flip a maximization by negating \(c\); flip inequality direction by multiplying by \(-1\); replace a free variable by the difference of two nonnegative ones (\(x = x^+ - x^-\)); turn inequalities into equalities with slack variables (\(a^\top x \le b \Rightarrow a^\top x + s = b,\ s\ge 0\)).

Why linearity mattersLinear objective + linear constraints ⇒ the feasible set is a convex polyhedron and every local optimum is global. That's why LPs with millions of variables solve in seconds while a single nonconvex constraint can make a tiny problem intractable. In interviews, actively look for ways to keep or make the model linear (see §5 for linearization tricks).
A first example, worked completely: the delivery-capacity LP

Business question. A delivery zone has two service types: standard orders yielding \$4 contribution margin each and priority ("express") orders yielding \$7. Each standard order consumes ~30 Dasher-minutes; express consumes ~45 (tighter routing). The zone has 6{,}000 Dasher-minutes available this evening, and merchant kitchen throughput caps express at 80 orders. Marketing committed at least 40 standard orders to a partner. How many of each should we accept to maximize margin?

Decision variables

\(x_s, x_e \ge 0\): number of standard / express orders accepted (treat as continuous for now).

Objective
$$\max \; 4x_s + 7x_e$$
Constraints
$$\begin{aligned} 30x_s + 45x_e &\le 6000 && \text{(Dasher-minutes)}\\ x_e &\le 80 && \text{(kitchen throughput)}\\ x_s &\ge 40 && \text{(partner commitment)}\\ x_s, x_e &\ge 0 \end{aligned}$$

Solving by geometry. With two variables, plot the feasible region; the optimum sits at a vertex. Candidate vertices: intersect Dasher-minutes with the kitchen cap: \(x_e=80,\ 30x_s=6000-3600=2400 \Rightarrow x_s=80\). Check the corner \((x_s, x_e) = (80, 80)\): objective \(=4(80)+7(80)=880\). Compare with putting everything into standard: \(x_s = 200, x_e = 0\): objective \(=800\). And the minimum-commitment corner \(x_s=40\): \(45x_e \le 4800 \Rightarrow x_e = 80\) (kitchen binds first), objective \(=160+560=720\). Optimum: \(\boxed{x_s=80,\ x_e=80}\), margin \$880. Both the Dasher-minutes and kitchen constraints are binding (hold with equality); the partner commitment is slack.

Interview tipKnowing which constraints bind is the doorway to duality (§4): binding constraints have positive shadow prices — "an extra Dasher-minute is worth about 8.9 cents to us tonight; extra kitchen capacity is worth \$3 per express slot." Saying this sentence in an interview signals real OR fluency.

3Geometry & how solvers actually work

The fundamental theorem: optima live at vertices

The feasible region of an LP is a convex polyhedron. Since the objective \(c^\top x\) is linear, its level sets are parallel hyperplanes — sliding a hyperplane as far as possible in the improving direction, the last contact with the polyhedron is at a vertex (or a whole face, when multiple optima exist). Consequences:

  • If an LP has a finite optimum, at least one optimal solution is a vertex (a basic feasible solution — at most \(m\) nonzero variables where \(m\) is the number of constraints).
  • Searching only vertices is enough — this is the basis of the simplex method.
  • Vertices of many network-structured LPs are naturally integral (§7) — the deep reason assignment and flow problems are "easy".
Simplex vs. interior-point vs. first-order methods

Simplex

Walks vertex-to-vertex along edges, each step swapping one variable into the "basis" while improving the objective. Exponential worst case, but superb in practice; its killer feature is warm starting — after a small data change, re-solving from the previous basis is nearly free. That's why simplex dominates in re-solve-every-minute production loops (like dispatch).

Interior-point (barrier)

Moves through the interior of the polyhedron following a "central path", using Newton steps on a barrier-augmented problem. Polynomial time, excels on very large sparse LPs, but doesn't warm-start well. Commercial solvers (Gurobi, CPLEX) run barrier and simplex concurrently and return whichever finishes first.

First-order / specialized methods

For enormous or structured problems: network simplex for flow problems, the Hungarian algorithm (\(O(n^3)\)) for pure assignment, auction algorithms, and modern first-order LP solvers (e.g., PDLP) for problems too big to factorize.

DoorDash connectionDoorDash's dispatch engine (DeepRed) publicly noted that formulating Dasher–order matching as a MIP solved with a commercial solver was up to 10× faster than the Hungarian algorithm at their scale, and far more flexible — batching, delay decisions and side constraints are trivial to add to a MIP but break specialized algorithms. Great example to cite when discussing exact-method tradeoffs.

4Duality, shadow prices & sensitivity

The dual: the same problem seen as pricing

Every LP (the primal) has a companion LP (the dual) built from the same data. For the primal in standard form:

Primal
$$\begin{aligned}\min_x\ & c^\top x\\ \text{s.t. } & Ax \ge b\\ & x\ge 0\end{aligned}$$
Dual
$$\begin{aligned}\max_y\ & b^\top y\\ \text{s.t. } & A^\top y \le c\\ & y\ge 0\end{aligned}$$

Mechanics to remember: one dual variable per primal constraint, one dual constraint per primal variable; the primal's right-hand side \(b\) becomes the dual's objective; the primal's costs \(c\) become the dual's right-hand side. Sense rules (for a min primal): a \(\ge\) constraint ⇒ dual variable \(\ge 0\); an \(=\) constraint ⇒ dual variable free; a \(\le\) constraint ⇒ dual variable \(\le 0\).

The economic story (this is what interviews probe)

The dual variable \(y_i\) is the shadow price of constraint \(i\): the rate at which the optimal objective improves per unit increase of \(b_i\), valid within a sensitivity range. In the §2 example, the dual value on Dasher-minutes answers: "what is one more Dasher-minute of supply worth tonight?" That is exactly the quantity you'd use to decide incentive spend.

  • Weak duality: any dual-feasible \(y\) gives a bound: \(b^\top y \le c^\top x\) for any primal-feasible \(x\). Duals certify solution quality.
  • Strong duality: if either problem has a finite optimum, both do, and optimal values are equal. (For LPs — not generally for MIPs, which is where the "duality gap" language comes from.)
  • Complementary slackness: at optimality, \(y_i \cdot (\text{slack}_i) = 0\) and \(x_j \cdot (\text{reduced cost}_j) = 0\). In words: a constraint with slack has price zero; a resource has positive price only if fully used.
Worked dual: pricing Dasher capacity

Take the §2 LP (drop the \(x_s \ge 40\) constraint, which was slack anyway, to keep it clean):

$$\begin{aligned}\max\ & 4x_s + 7x_e \quad \text{s.t.}\quad 30x_s + 45x_e \le 6000,\quad x_e \le 80,\quad x \ge 0\end{aligned}$$

The dual has one variable per constraint: \(u\) (per Dasher-minute) and \(v\) (per express slot):

$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & 6000u + 80v \\ \text{s.t. } & 30u \ge 4 && \text{(covering the value of a standard order)}\\ & 45u + v \ge 7 && \text{(covering the value of an express order)}\\ & u, v \ge 0\end{aligned}$$

Interpretation: find internal prices for Dasher-minutes and kitchen slots such that every order's resource bundle is priced at at least its margin, minimizing the total imputed value of resources. Solving: both primal constraints bind, so both dual constraints for the used variables bind: \(30u = 4 \Rightarrow u = 0.1\overline{3}\); \(45u + v = 7 \Rightarrow v = 1\). Dual objective \(= 6000(0.1\overline{3}) + 80(1) = 800 + 80 = 880\) — exactly the primal optimum (strong duality). So: a marginal Dasher-minute is worth ≈\$0.133 (≈\$8/hour of extra supply) and a marginal express kitchen slot is worth \$1.

Interview tipConcrete uses of duals you can cite: (1) incentive pricing — shadow price of the supply constraint tells you the break-even Dasher bonus; (2) bottleneck triage — rank constraints by shadow price to know which capacity to expand; (3) column generation — duals price out new candidate routes/batches (§12); (4) reduced costs — tell you how much a currently-unused option's cost must drop before it enters the solution.
Reduced costs, sensitivity ranges & Lagrangian view

Reduced cost

For a nonbasic (zero) variable \(x_j\), the reduced cost \(\bar c_j = c_j - A_j^\top y\) is how much its objective coefficient must improve before it becomes worth using. "Why doesn't the model ever assign this Dasher across town? Its reduced cost is +\$3 — the assignment would need to be \$3 cheaper to enter."

Sensitivity / ranging

Shadow prices are valid only while the optimal basis stays optimal — solvers report ranges for each RHS \(b_i\) and cost \(c_j\). Say this in interviews: "the dual price holds locally; for a large capacity change I'd re-solve rather than extrapolate."

Lagrangian connection

Duality = relaxing constraints into the objective with multiplier prices: \(L(x,y) = c^\top x + y^\top(b - Ax)\), and the dual function \(g(y)=\min_{x\ge0} L(x,y)\) lower-bounds the primal. This view generalizes beyond LP (Lagrangian relaxation for MIPs, §12) and is the cleanest way to explain why duals are prices: \(y_i\) is the penalty rate at which constraint \(i\) is "bought out".

Common trapFor MIPs, strong duality fails: LP-relaxation duals are commonly used as approximate prices, but there is generally an integrality gap. If asked "what's the shadow price in your MIP?", the sharp answer: "MIPs don't have exact shadow prices; I'd quote the LP-relaxation dual as an approximation, or re-solve with \(b_i \pm 1\) and difference the objectives."

5Constraint modeling patterns (the linearization toolbox)

Binary variables & the big-M pattern

Binary variables \(z \in \{0,1\}\) turn LPs into MIPs but unlock logic. The workhorse is big-M: linking a continuous quantity to an on/off decision.

"x can be positive only if z = 1" (fixed-charge / setup pattern)
$$x \le M z, \qquad x \ge 0,\; z\in\{0,1\}$$
"If z = 1, constraint must hold" (indicator)
$$a^\top x \le b + M(1-z) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad z=1 \Rightarrow a^\top x \le b$$

Example — fixed cost: opening a dark-store costs \$50k plus \$2 per order served. Let \(z=1\) if opened, \(x\) = orders served, capacity 10k: cost \(= 50000z + 2x\) with \(x \le 10000\,z\). If \(z=0\), \(x\) is forced to 0.

Common trap — and a favorite interview probeChoose \(M\) as small as validly possible (here \(M=10000\), the true capacity — never \(10^9\)). Loose big-Ms make the LP relaxation weak: the relaxation can set \(z = x/M \approx 0.001\) and "pay" almost no fixed cost, so branch-and-bound gets poor bounds and slows dramatically. Mentioning tight big-Ms (or indicator constraints, which solvers like Gurobi support natively) is a strong signal.
Logical conditions as linear constraints
EnglishConstraint (all \(z\in\{0,1\}\))
At most one of A, B\(z_A + z_B \le 1\)

B iff (A1 and A2)\(z_B \le z_{A1},\; z_B \le z_{A2},\; z_B \ge z_{A1}+z_{A2}-1\)

Count: at least \(k\) of \(n\) conditions\(\sum_i z_i \ge k\)

Example — either/or in scheduling: two deliveries assigned to the same Dasher must not overlap: either \(j\) finishes before \(k\) starts or vice versa. With start times \(t_j, t_k\), service times \(s_j, s_k\), and a sequencing binary \(z\): \(t_j + s_j \le t_k + M(1-z)\) and \(t_k + s_k \le t_j + Mz\). This disjunctive pattern is the backbone of scheduling MIPs.

Linearizing min / max / absolute value / products

Max and min in the objective

Minimize a maximum (makespan / fairness): introduce \(T\)
$$\min\; T \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \sum_j w_j x_{ij} \le T \;\;\forall i$$

This is exactly how you balance workloads across Dashers ("minimize the longest route"). Symmetrically, maximize a minimum for fairness floors. Caution: minimizing a max is easy (convex); maximizing a max or putting a max on the wrong side of a \(\le\) needs binaries.

Absolute value

$$\min |u| \;\Rightarrow\; \min\, (u^+ + u^-),\quad u = u^+ - u^-,\; u^\pm \ge 0$$

Used constantly for deviation penalties: "as close to target as possible" \(\min |x - \tau|\). Valid when the objective pushes both parts down (minimization); in other positions it needs binaries.

Products

  • \(z_1 z_2\) (binary × binary): \(w \le z_1,\ w \le z_2,\ w \ge z_1 + z_2 - 1\).
  • \(z \cdot x\) (binary × continuous, \(0\le x\le U\)): \(w \le Uz,\ w \le x,\ w \ge x - U(1-z),\ w \ge 0\) (McCormick).
  • \(x_1 x_2\) (continuous × continuous): genuinely nonconvex — avoid; discretize one variable or reformulate.

Piecewise-linear functions

Tiered incentives, volume discounts, soft-then-hard lateness costs: model with convex combination (λ) formulation over breakpoints \((a_k, f(a_k))\): \(x = \sum_k \lambda_k a_k\), \(y = \sum_k \lambda_k f(a_k)\), \(\sum_k \lambda_k = 1\), \(\lambda \ge 0\) plus SOS2 (or adjacency binaries) so at most two consecutive \(\lambda_k\) are positive. Shortcut worth saying: if the piecewise function is convex and being minimized, no binaries are needed — just add one linear constraint per piece: \(y \ge m_k x + c_k\ \forall k\).

Sets, indices & the discipline of notation

Clean formulations start with clean notation. Adopt this template and use it verbatim in the interview:

Sets

\(I\) = Dashers, \(J\) = orders, \(T\) = time periods, \(K\) = zones…

Parameters (data — known, lowercase)

\(c_{ij}\) = cost of assigning \(i\) to \(j\); \(d_{jt}\) = demand; \(q_i\) = capacity…

Decision variables (unknown — declare domain!)

\(x_{ij} \in \{0,1\}\); \(y_t \ge 0\); \(T \in \mathbb{R}\)…

  • Always declare variable domains (binary / integer / continuous, bounds). Forgetting \(x \ge 0\) is the classic whiteboard bug.
  • Quantify every constraint: \(\forall i \in I\), \(\forall t \in T\). An unquantified constraint is ambiguous.
  • Sanity-check units in each constraint (minutes vs. orders vs. dollars) — catches most formulation errors.
  • State model size: "\(|I|\times|J|\) binaries ≈ 200 × 500 = 100k — fine for a modern solver with a time limit."

6Penalties, soft constraints & infeasibility

Hard vs. soft constraints — the elastic pattern

A hard constraint can never be violated (physics, law, safety). A soft constraint expresses a strong preference (SLA targets, fairness goals) — violating it is bad but sometimes optimal. Softening = add a nonnegative violation slack and charge it in the objective:

Hard
$$\text{delivery time}_j \le 45 \;\;\forall j$$
Soft (elastic)
$$\text{delivery time}_j \le 45 + v_j,\qquad v_j \ge 0,\qquad \min\;\Big(\cdots + \sum_j \rho\, v_j\Big)$$

\(\rho\) is the penalty rate (dollars per minute late). Real dispatch systems soften almost everything except physical/logical constraints, because live marketplaces are routinely over-constrained (demand spike + Dasher shortage ⇒ some order will be late; the model should choose which one, at minimum damage — not return INFEASIBLE at 7pm on a Friday).

Interview framingA crisp taxonomy to recite: "I classify constraints into (1) physical/logical — hard; (2) business policy — soft with high penalty; (3) preferences — soft with low penalty or objective terms." Then ask the interviewer which category each requirement belongs to. This question-asking is explicitly graded.
Choosing penalty weights & multi-objective handling

With several goals (speed, cost, Dasher earnings, freshness) you have three standard moves:

  1. Weighted sum: \(\min\; \alpha\,(\text{delivery time}) + \beta\,(\text{cost}) + \gamma\,(\text{lateness})\). Simple, differentiable business tuning; weights should be set in common units (dollarize: a minute of customer waiting ≈ \$w based on churn/LTV analysis — say this!).
  2. Lexicographic / hierarchical: optimize goal 1; fix it (within tolerance \(\epsilon\)); optimize goal 2 subject to that. Good when priorities are strict; solvers support multi-objective natively.
  3. ε-constraint: optimize one objective, bound the others as constraints ("min cost s.t. p95 lateness ≤ 5 min"). Best when the business gives you SLA-style targets; sweeping ε traces the Pareto frontier.

Practical rules for penalty magnitudes:

  • Penalties must dominate the gains from violating them — but not by orders of magnitude, or the LP becomes ill-conditioned and the solver's feasibility tolerances start to bite.
  • Tiered penalties model reality better: first 5 min late ≈ mild; beyond 15 min ≈ severe (piecewise-linear convex ⇒ no binaries needed when minimized, §5).
  • A soft constraint's optimal violation being 0 with positive slack price ⇒ effectively hard at optimum; monitor violation levels over time as a health metric.
Diagnosing infeasibility like a pro

When a model returns infeasible, the interview-grade answer has three moves:

  1. Elastic relaxation: add penalized slacks to all suspect constraint groups, re-solve, and read which slacks are positive — those constraints are the conflict. This also converts "no answer" into "least-bad answer", usually what production needs.
  2. IIS (Irreducible Infeasible Subsystem): solvers can extract a minimal set of mutually conflicting constraints (model.computeIIS() in Gurobi) — great for debugging data errors like a zone whose demand exceeds any possible supply.
  3. Check data before blaming the model: most production infeasibilities are data bugs — negative capacities, duplicate orders, stale Dasher locations.
Common trapDon't say "I'd just relax constraints until it's feasible." Say which constraints are legitimately soft (SLAs, preferences) and which must stay hard (a Dasher can't be in two places), and that the penalty ordering encodes business priority for who gets hurt first under scarcity.

7MIP, LP relaxation & branch-and-bound

What changes when variables must be integer

A mixed-integer program (MIP) is an LP where some variables are integer/binary. The feasible set becomes a lattice of points inside a polyhedron — nonconvex — and the problem becomes NP-hard. Two central objects:

MIP
$$z^{MIP} = \min\{\,c^\top x : Ax \ge b,\; x \ge 0,\; x_j \in \mathbb{Z}\ \forall j \in \mathcal{I}\,\}$$
LP relaxation — drop integrality
$$z^{LP} = \min\{\,c^\top x : Ax \ge b,\; x \ge 0\,\} \;\le\; z^{MIP} \quad(\text{for minimization})$$
  • The LP relaxation gives a bound (lower for min, upper for max) and is the anchor of everything: branch-and-bound prunes with it, the MIP gap is measured against it, and its duals price resources approximately.
  • Integrality gap \(= z^{MIP} - z^{LP}\): how much lying the relaxation does. Tight formulations (small gap) solve fast — this is why formulation choice matters more than solver settings.
  • Rounding a fractional LP solution is not generally valid (can be infeasible or far from optimal) — but rounding + repair is a legitimate, nameable heuristic.

When you get integrality for free

If the constraint matrix is totally unimodular (TU) and the RHS is integer, every LP vertex is already integral: assignment, transportation, max-flow, min-cost flow, shortest path. Practical takeaway you should say out loud: "pure assignment doesn't even need integer variables — I can solve the LP and get a binary answer. It's the batching/side constraints that force a true MIP."

Branch-and-bound (and cut) — how the solver thinks
  1. Solve the LP relaxation. If the solution happens to be integral — done.
  2. Branch: pick a fractional \(x_j = 2.4\); create two children: \(x_j \le 2\) and \(x_j \ge 3\). This partitions the search space without losing any integer solution.
  3. Bound: each node's LP value is a bound for its subtree. Maintain the incumbent (best integer solution found).
  4. Prune a node if: its LP is infeasible; its bound is worse than the incumbent; or its LP is integral (update incumbent).
  5. Cuts (branch-and-cut): add valid inequalities that slice off fractional LP points without removing integer ones (Gomory, cover, flow-cover cuts) — tightens bounds, shrinks the tree.
  6. Heuristics inside the solver: rounding, diving, feasibility pump, RINS give good incumbents early — why solvers often find a 1%-gap solution in seconds and spend minutes proving optimality.
MIP gap (what the solver log shows)
$$\text{gap} = \frac{|z^{incumbent} - z^{bound}|}{|z^{incumbent}|} \qquad \text{e.g. stop at gap} \le 1\%\ \text{or } t \ge 1\text{s}$$
Production wisdom to volunteerReal-time systems run MIPs with a time limit + gap tolerance and accept the incumbent: "optimal enough, on time." Also: warm-start from the previous cycle's solution (dispatch re-solves every minute with 95% unchanged data); fix-and-optimize (freeze most variables, re-optimize the changed neighborhood) for speed.
What makes MIPs slow — and formulation strength
  • Weak LP relaxations (loose big-Ms, §5) ⇒ bad bounds ⇒ huge trees. Fix: tight constants, indicator constraints, or stronger formulations.
  • Symmetry: interchangeable Dashers/vehicles make the solver explore mirror-image solutions. Fix: symmetry-breaking constraints (order the vehicles: \(u_1 \ge u_2 \ge \cdots\)) or aggregate identical entities into counts.
  • Big-M disjunctions everywhere (scheduling) ⇒ notoriously weak; consider time-indexed formulations or CP (constraint programming) alternatives.
  • Formulation strength example worth memorizing — facility location. Aggregate: \(\sum_j x_{ij} \le n\, z_i\) (one constraint, weak). Disaggregate: \(x_{ij} \le z_i\ \forall j\) (many constraints, but the LP relaxation is much tighter — solves faster despite being "bigger"). "Bigger but tighter beats smaller but weaker" is an expert-level line.

8Canonical OR problems — the pattern library

Assignment problem — the atom of dispatch

Pattern: match \(n\) agents to \(n\) tasks, one each, minimizing total cost. DoorDash face: which Dasher takes which order.

$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & \sum_{i\in I}\sum_{j\in J} c_{ij} x_{ij}\\ \text{s.t. } & \sum_{j} x_{ij} \le 1 \ \ \forall i && \text{(each Dasher ≤ 1 order)}\\ & \sum_{i} x_{ij} = 1 \ \ \forall j && \text{(each order exactly 1 Dasher)}\\ & x_{ij} \in \{0,1\}\end{aligned}$$
  • TU matrix ⇒ LP relaxation is integral; solvable by Hungarian \(O(n^3)\), min-cost flow, or as an LP/MIP.
  • \(c_{ij}\) is where ML meets OR: predicted delivery time, travel time, prep-time readiness, acceptance probability, all blended into a score. The MIP consumes ML outputs as coefficients.
  • Unbalanced case: add dummy rows/columns or use \(\le\) constraints with an "unassigned" penalty column — i.e., leaving an order unassigned is allowed at high cost (soft constraint pattern!).
Transportation & min-cost network flow

Pattern: ship from supply nodes to demand nodes at minimum cost. DoorDash face: repositioning Dasher supply across zones; moving inventory to dark stores.

$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & \sum_{s,d} c_{sd}\, x_{sd}\\ \text{s.t. } & \sum_d x_{sd} \le S_s\ \ \forall s && \text{(supply)}\\ & \sum_s x_{sd} \ge D_d\ \ \forall d && \text{(demand)}\\ & x \ge 0\end{aligned}$$

Generalizes to min-cost flow on any network with arc capacities and node balance constraints \(\sum_{\text{in}} x - \sum_{\text{out}} x = b_v\); includes shortest path and max-flow as special cases. All polynomial, all integral with integer data. Recognizing flow structure inside a business problem ("this is just a flow problem — polynomial, no need for heuristics") is a big credibility win.

Knapsack & budget allocation

Pattern: choose items with values \(v_j\) and weights \(w_j\) under capacity \(B\). DoorDash face: which promotions/incentives to fund under a budget; which features to include under latency budget.

$$\max \sum_j v_j x_j \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \sum_j w_j x_j \le B,\quad x_j \in \{0,1\}$$
  • LP relaxation: sort by \(v_j/w_j\) (bang-per-buck), fill greedily; at most one fractional item. Greedy on ratios is near-optimal when items are small vs. budget — the justification for simple incentive-allocation heuristics.
  • DP in \(O(nB)\) — pseudo-polynomial; fine for modest budgets.
  • Multi-dimensional (several resource constraints) and multiple-choice (pick one option per group) variants cover most marketing/budgeting problems.
Set covering / packing / partitioning — the shift & batching workhorse
Covering (≥) — e.g., every hour must be staffed
$$\min \sum_k c_k y_k \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \sum_k a_{tk}\, y_k \ge d_t\ \ \forall t,\quad y_k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$$

\(a_{tk}=1\) if shift template \(k\) covers hour \(t\); \(y_k\) = number of Dashers on template \(k\).

Partitioning (=) — e.g., every order in exactly one batch
$$\min \sum_k c_k y_k \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \sum_k a_{jk}\, y_k = 1\ \ \forall j,\quad y_k \in \{0,1\}$$

The column \(k\) is a whole feasible object (a shift, a batch, a route) and \(a_{\cdot k}\) records what it covers. This "columns = candidate plans" view is the gateway to column generation (§12): you don't enumerate all batches — you generate promising ones on the fly using dual prices. Crew scheduling, batching, and VRP are all solved this way at scale.

Facility location

Pattern: open facilities (fixed cost) and assign customers (service cost). DoorDash face: dark kitchens, DashMart placement, zone depot siting.

$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & \sum_i f_i z_i + \sum_{i,j} c_{ij} x_{ij}\\ \text{s.t. } & \sum_i x_{ij} = 1\ \forall j;\qquad x_{ij} \le z_i\ \forall i,j;\qquad \sum_j d_j x_{ij} \le Q_i z_i\ \forall i\\ & z_i \in \{0,1\},\ x_{ij} \ge 0 \ (\text{or } \in \{0,1\})\end{aligned}$$

Note both the disaggregated linking \(x_{ij} \le z_i\) (strong formulation, §7) and the capacity link. The \(p\)-median variant fixes the number of facilities (\(\sum_i z_i = p\)) instead of fixed costs.

TSP & Vehicle Routing (VRP/VRPTW)

TSP: one vehicle, visit all stops, minimize tour length. Binary arc variables \(x_{uv}\), degree-2 constraints, plus subtour elimination — either exponentially many cuts \(\sum_{u,v \in S} x_{uv} \le |S|-1\ \forall S\) (added lazily) or MTZ ordering variables \(u_v\): \(u_u - u_v + n\,x_{uv} \le n-1\) (compact but weak).

VRP adds multiple vehicles + capacities; VRPTW adds time windows via the disjunctive/time-propagation constraints:

$$t_v \ge t_u + s_u + \tau_{uv} - M(1 - x_{uv}), \qquad e_v \le t_v \le \ell_v$$

(arrival time propagation when arc \(u\to v\) is used; window \([e_v,\ell_v]\)). Practical hierarchy to recite: exact MIP ≤ ~50–100 stops; column generation / branch-and-price to a few hundred; beyond that, metaheuristics (savings, LNS via OR-Tools) — which is what all delivery companies actually run. Food delivery nuance: batches are small (2–4 orders), so per-Dasher routing is nearly trivial — the hard part is which orders to group, not the tour order.

Bin packing, scheduling & newsvendor (rapid fire)

Bin packing

Pack items of size \(w_j\) into fewest capacity-\(Q\) bins: \(y_k\) = bin used, \(x_{jk}\) = item in bin. \(\min \sum_k y_k\), \(\sum_j w_j x_{jk} \le Q y_k\), \(\sum_k x_{jk} = 1\). First-Fit-Decreasing uses ≤ \(\tfrac{11}{9}\)OPT + 1 bins — name it as the go-to heuristic. Face: orders into Dasher bags/vehicles, catering into vans.

Scheduling

Machines = Dashers/kitchen lines, jobs = orders with release times (food-ready) and due dates (promise time). Objectives: makespan \(C_{\max}\), total weighted tardiness \(\sum w_j T_j\) with \(T_j \ge C_j - d_j,\ T_j \ge 0\) — note tardiness is a penalized slack, tying back to §6.

Newsvendor — one-shot stochastic capacity

$$q^* = F^{-1}\!\Big(\frac{c_u}{c_u + c_o}\Big) \quad\text{(critical fractile)}$$

Order quantity under uncertain demand \(F\), underage cost \(c_u\) (lost sale / late order), overage cost \(c_o\) (idle Dasher-hours). Face: how many Dashers to mobilize ahead of a demand forecast — if an idle Dasher-hour costs \$5 of guarantees and an unserved order costs \$15, staff to the 75th percentile of demand. This one formula elegantly connects forecasting to staffing and is a superb thing to drop when supply planning comes up.

9The DoorDash problem landscape

The marketplace in one picture — and where OR lives

DoorDash is a three-sided marketplace: consumers (want fast, cheap, reliable), Dashers (want earnings, flexibility), merchants (want volume, fresh handoff). Key physical fact: food decays — an order sitting on a counter loses quality, so timing constraints are tighter than parcel logistics; and supply is self-scheduled — you influence Dashers with incentives, you don't command them.

The three tensions every answer should acknowledge
  1. Efficiency vs. quality: batching cuts cost-per-delivery but risks lateness and cold food. Every batching answer must include a lateness/quality guard (constraint or penalty).
  2. Now vs. later: greedy myopic matching wastes options — the best Dasher for this order may be finishing a delivery 2 minutes away. Hence delay decisions, rolling horizons, and predicted future supply in the objective.
  3. System vs. individual: optimizing global throughput can starve specific Dashers of earnings or specific merchants of coverage — fairness floors (max-min, §5) and acceptance-probability terms matter, and Dashers can decline offers (model acceptance probability, or re-offer loops).
Interview tipUncertainty is everywhere (prep times, travel, acceptance). You rarely need full stochastic programming in a first answer. 10The formulation playbook — a repeatable 8-step procedure

The 8 steps (memorize this — it structures your whole hour)
  1. Clarify the business goal (2–3 min). Ask: What are we optimizing — cost, speed, revenue, some blend? Over what horizon? What decisions are actually controllable? What's the scale (orders/minute, Dashers/zone)? What's the latency budget for solving? Asking questions is explicitly on the rubric.
  2. State assumptions out loud. "I'll assume travel times are known point estimates from an ML model, single region, and I'll start deterministic — then discuss uncertainty." Assumptions are not weaknesses; unstated assumptions are.
  3. Define sets & parameters. \(I\) Dashers, \(J\) orders, data \(c_{ij}, d_j, Q_i\)… Distinguish data (known) from decisions (unknown).
  4. Define decision variables with domains. The key creative act. Ask yourself: what is the atomic decision? Assign? Open? Route? Price? Delay? Choose granularity (order-level vs. batch-level vs. zone-level) to match the question and the solve-time budget.
  5. Write the objective. One line, in dollars if possible. If multiple goals: weighted sum with dollarized weights, or ε-constraint (§6). Say which and why.
  6. Write constraints in named groups. (a) assignment/logic, (b) capacity, (c) time/quality-of-service, (d) budget/policy, (e) variable domains. Quantify each with \(\forall\). Mark which are soft.
  7. Sanity-check. Units per constraint; a tiny instance mentally (2 Dashers, 3 orders); edge cases — what if no feasible assignment? (⇒ add unassigned-penalty option); count variables/constraints and state whether solver + time limit is realistic.
  8. Discuss solution strategy & validation. Exact MIP with gap/time limit? Relaxation-based heuristic? Decomposition? Then: how you'd validate (backtest vs. historical decisions, simulation, shadow mode, A/B) and what business metric moves.

11Six worked DoorDash-style formulations

Example 1 — Real-time Dasher–order matching (the classic)

Prompt. "Every minute, we have a set of unassigned orders and available Dashers in a region. Design the optimization that decides who delivers what."

Clarify: objective = blend of delivery speed and Dasher efficiency; Dashers may decline; leaving an order briefly unassigned is allowed; ML gives \(\hat t_{ij}\) = predicted delivery time if \(i\) serves \(j\), and food-ready times. Scale: ~10²–10³ orders/Dashers per region per solve, ≤1s budget.

Sets & parameters

\(I\): available Dashers; \(J\): pending orders; \(c_{ij}\): assignment cost (weighted blend: predicted delivery duration, Dasher travel/wait, tip/earnings balance), \(p_j\): penalty for deferring order \(j\) this round (grows with order age).

Decision variables

\(x_{ij} \in \{0,1\}\): offer order \(j\) to Dasher \(i\); \(u_j \in \{0,1\}\): order \(j\) deferred to next solve.

Model
$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & \sum_{i,j} c_{ij}\,x_{ij} + \sum_j p_j\, u_j\\ \text{s.t. } & \textstyle\sum_i x_{ij} + u_j = 1 && \forall j\ \ \text{(served or deferred)}\\ & \textstyle\sum_j x_{ij} \le 1 && \forall i\ \ \text{(one offer per Dasher)}\\ & x_{ij} = 0 && \forall (i,j)\ \text{infeasible pairs (too far, arrives before food ready + buffer)}\end{aligned}$$

Walking through the model, decision by decision

Why these variables? The atomic decision a dispatcher makes is "offer order \(j\) to Dasher \(i\) — yes or no", hence one binary \(x_{ij}\) per plausible pair. The second variable \(u_j\) exists because "assign nobody right now" is also a legitimate decision, and often a good one: the marketplace refreshes every minute, and a much better Dasher may free up 90 seconds from now. Without \(u_j\), the model would be forced into bad matches just to stay feasible.
Why this objective? \(c_{ij}\) is a single dollarized score per pair — concretely something like \(c_{ij} = w_1\,\hat t^{\,deliv}_{ij} + w_2\,\hat t^{\,travel}_{ij} + w_3\,(\text{Dasher wait at store})\), where the \(\hat t\)'s come from ML models and the weights convert minutes into dollars (a minute of customer waiting ≈ churn-derived cost; a minute of Dasher time ≈ pay rate). The deferral penalty \(p_j\) competes in the same units, so deferral wins exactly when \(\min_i c_{ij} > p_j\): the best available match costs more than waiting one more cycle.
Constraint 1 — served or deferred. \(\sum_i x_{ij} + u_j = 1\) says every order gets exactly one disposition this cycle. The slack is built into the equality — this is the elastic pattern of §6, and it guarantees the model can never return INFEASIBLE, which matters at 7pm on a Friday.
Constraint 2 — one offer per Dasher. \(\sum_j x_{ij} \le 1\). It is \(\le\), not \(=\), because leaving a Dasher idle is allowed — and correct when they're badly positioned for everything currently open.
Sparsification. Never build all \(|I|\times|J|\) variables. Pre-filter to pairs within a travel-time radius, where the Dasher's arrival wouldn't badly precede food-ready time. In a region with 300 Dashers × 400 orders, filtering typically cuts 120k pairs to a few thousand — the difference between a 50 ms and a 5 s solve.
How \(p_j\) creates the delay policy. Make \(p_j\) increase with order age (e.g., a linear ramp after 2 minutes). A young order facing only poor matches gets deferred; as it ages, \(p_j\) climbs until some assignment becomes cheaper than waiting. One parameter yields a principled "wait for a better match" behavior — no separate delay logic needed.
Sanity checks to say out loud. Units: everything in dollars. Tiny instance: 1 Dasher, 2 orders ⇒ one order must defer (Constraint 2 caps offers) — correct behavior. Solve time: assignment-like structure, near-integral LP relaxation, warm-startable ⇒ comfortably sub-second.

Follow-up questions & strong answers

Dashers can decline offers. Doesn't your model assume acceptance?
Yes — and the fix has a reactive and a proactive half, used together. Reactive: the loop re-solves every minute, so a declined order simply reappears next cycle with a higher \(p_j\) (it aged). Proactive: discount each match by predicted acceptance probability \(\hat a_{ij}\) — use expected effective cost (e.g., inflate \(c_{ij}\) for low-acceptance pairs) so the model avoids offers likely to bounce. Operationally I'd also cap re-offers per order and escalate pay after each decline: repeated declines signal a mispriced offer, and every bounce costs 1–2 minutes of customer latency.
This runs per region every minute. What breaks at 10× scale, and what do you do about it?
Variable count grows with Dashers × orders per region, so the levers in order: (1) sharper pair filtering — smaller radius, top-\(k\) nearest Dashers per order; (2) warm-start from the previous cycle — 95% of marketplace state is unchanged minute-to-minute, so the solver mostly re-confirms the prior basis; (3) split into independent geographic subproblems (the constraint matrix is already block-diagonal across regions). Only if all that fails would I go heuristic — and I'd keep the MIP running offline as the benchmark that measures the heuristic's optimality gap.
How would you extend this to batching — one Dasher carrying multiple orders?
Change the unit of assignment from an order to a batch. Generate candidate batches (singletons, pairs, triples passing locality and lateness screens), then assign Dashers to batches with a partitioning constraint ensuring each order lands in exactly one selected batch — that's Example 2. The key insight to voice: the matching model's structure survives; only the definition of a "column" changes from one order to a small bundle.
Your model optimizes each minute myopically. Doesn't it miss the future — say, a dinner rush starting in 20 minutes?
Per-cycle re-solving is a rolling horizon, which absorbs most uncertainty, but it is indeed myopic about predictable future imbalance. Two pragmatic upgrades: (1) add a future-value term to \(c_{ij}\) that penalizes stranding a Dasher in a zone forecast to go quiet — an ML-estimated opportunity cost playing the role of a value function; (2) make the deferral penalty demand-aware, deferring more aggressively when supply is forecast to improve. Full RL/stochastic dispatch exists, but I'd demand simulation evidence that it beats the rolling-horizon baseline before taking on that complexity.
What about Dasher fairness — couldn't some Dashers get starved of earnings?
Three escalating tools: (1) a soft term in \(c_{ij}\) favoring Dashers with low recent earnings — cheap and tunable; (2) a hard anti-starvation rule — no active Dasher goes more than \(T\) minutes without an offer, implemented as a high-priority bonus on starved pairs; (3) max-min fairness — maximize minimum session earnings via the \(\min T\) pattern from §5. The stronger the guarantee, the more total efficiency you give up, so I'd trace the fairness–efficiency Pareto frontier and let the business choose the operating point — that framing itself scores points.
Example 2 — Order batching (set partitioning + column generation story)

Prompt. "When should we give one Dasher multiple orders, and how do we choose the groups?"

Clarify: batches of ≤3; orders batchable if pickup locations close and routes compatible; guard against lateness. Two-level structure: generate candidate batches, then select.

Step 1 — generate candidate batches (offline logic / heuristic)

\(K\): candidate batches — singletons plus pairs/triples passing screens (same merchant cluster, detour ≤ threshold, predicted lateness ≤ cap). For each \(k\): cost \(c_k\) (total predicted route time + weighted expected lateness) and incidence \(a_{jk} = 1\) if order \(j \in k\).

Step 2 — select batches: set partitioning
$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & \sum_{k\in K} c_k\, y_k \\ \text{s.t. } & \sum_k a_{jk}\, y_k = 1 \quad \forall j\in J && \text{(each order in exactly one chosen batch)}\\ & \sum_k y_k \le |I| && \text{(enough Dashers)}\\ & y_k \in \{0,1\}\end{aligned}$$

Walking through the model, decision by decision

Why two levels? The naive formulation — binaries for "orders \(j\) and \(j'\) share a Dasher" — drowns in pairwise consistency constraints and quadratic interactions. The professional move is to change what a variable means: let each variable represent an entire candidate batch. All the messy feasibility logic (route compatibility, lateness) gets checked once, at generation time, outside the optimization; the optimization then only answers "which of these pre-validated bundles do we pick?" This "columns = whole plans" trick is the same one behind airline crew scheduling.
Generation step in practice. For each merchant cluster, enumerate singleton batches (always), plus pairs/triples of orders whose pickups are within, say, 200 m and whose combined best route adds ≤ \(x\) minutes of detour and ≤ \(y\) minutes of predicted lateness for any member. For 2–3 order batches over locality-pruned neighborhoods, this is thousands of candidates, not millions — cheap. For each candidate, compute the best stop sequence (Example 6 — trivially enumerable at this size) to get its cost \(c_k\).
The partitioning constraint. \(\sum_k a_{jk} y_k = 1\) for every order \(j\): each order appears in exactly one selected batch. Since singletons are always candidates, feasibility is guaranteed — the model can always fall back to "no batching at all." That built-in fallback is worth pointing out; it's the same always-feasible design principle as Example 1's deferral variable.
The cost \(c_k\) carries the tradeoff. A pair batch has lower total Dasher time than two singletons (that's the efficiency win) but higher expected lateness (the quality cost). Put both into \(c_k\) in dollars: \(c_k = (\text{route time}) \cdot (\text{Dasher \$/min}) + \rho \cdot (\text{expected lateness minutes})\). The optimizer then batches exactly when efficiency gains beat quality costs — the business tradeoff lives in one tunable \(\rho\).
Capacity link. \(\sum_k y_k \le |I|\) keeps selected batches within available Dashers. In the full production version you'd instead assign specific Dashers to batches (\(x_{ik}\), merging Example 1's structure) because Dasher position affects each batch's cost — say this as the natural extension.

Follow-up questions & strong answers

Your candidate set could explode for dense downtown zones. What then?
That's the cue for column generation. Don't enumerate — iterate: solve the LP relaxation over the current (small) candidate pool; read the dual price \(\pi_j\) on each order's partitioning constraint (interpretable as "what the current solution implicitly pays to cover order \(j\)"); then run a pricing subroutine that searches for a new batch whose cost beats its members' combined dual prices, i.e., \(c_k - \sum_{j\in k}\pi_j < 0\). Add it, re-solve, repeat until no improving batch exists — at which point the LP is provably optimal over all possible batches, including ones never enumerated. For integer solutions this becomes branch-and-price; in practice at DoorDash-like scale, batches cap at 2–4 orders, so smart enumeration usually suffices — knowing when the fancy tool is unnecessary is part of the answer.
Should lateness protection be a hard rule or a penalty?
Both appear in practice, at different layers. Generation-time hard screen: never even create a batch predicted to make any member > 10 min late — keeps toxic options out of the model entirely and keeps the master problem clean. Selection-time soft penalty: within surviving candidates, charge expected lateness in \(c_k\) so the model still prefers gentler batches. The layered answer is stronger than picking one: hard screens bound worst-case harm; soft penalties optimize within the safe set. And mention monitoring: track realized lateness of batched vs. unbatched orders as the health metric for whether screens are calibrated.
Prep-time predictions are noisy. How does that change batching?
Batching couples orders, so one late kitchen now delays two or three customers — errors are correlated damage. Three mitigations: (1) use a conservative quantile (say p75) of predicted prep time in the lateness screen rather than the mean — asymmetric costs demand quantile inputs, the §8 newsvendor logic again; (2) prefer batching orders from the same merchant (one prep-time risk, not two independent ones); (3) leave re-sequencing freedom to the Dasher app at execution time — the plan adapts when reality diverges. If pushed further: simulate the batching policy against historical prep-time realizations and compare p90 lateness, not just averages.
How do you measure whether batching is actually good for the business?
Define the tension explicitly: batching raises deliveries-per-Dasher-hour (efficiency, cost side) but risks lateness and food quality (experience side). Offline: replay historical days under the batching policy vs. without, comparing cost per delivery, on-time rate, p90 lateness. Online: switchback experiment (randomize by region × time window — user-level randomization is invalid here because batching changes shared Dasher supply), with guardrails on cancellation rate, rating dips, and Dasher earnings per hour. The metric ladder answer — offline replay, then switchback with guardrails — is exactly what they want to hear.
Example 3 — Dasher shift scheduling / supply planning (set covering + newsvendor)

Prompt. "Given an hourly demand forecast for next week, decide how many Dasher shift slots to open per zone."

Clarify: forecast \(\hat d_{t}\) (orders/hour) with uncertainty; each Dasher-hour serves \(\mu\) orders (e.g., 2.5); shifts are templates (e.g., 11–14h, 17–21h); Dashers self-select, so "open slots" is an upper bound on show-ups — apply a show-rate factor \(\sigma_k\).

Sets & parameters

\(T\): hours; \(K\): shift templates, \(a_{tk}=1\) if template \(k\) covers hour \(t\); \(c_k\): expected cost per slot (guarantees/incentives); \(r_t\): required Dasher-hours \(= q_\alpha(\hat d_t)/\mu\) at service percentile \(\alpha\); \(v_t\): cost rate of unmet coverage.

Decisions & model

\(y_k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}\): slots opened on template \(k\); \(s_t \ge 0\): coverage shortfall.

$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & \sum_k c_k y_k + \sum_t v_t\, s_t\\ \text{s.t. } & \sum_k a_{tk}\,\sigma_k\, y_k + s_t \ge r_t \quad \forall t\\ & \sum_k y_k \le N^{max} \ \ \text{(recruitable pool)},\qquad y_k \le U_k \ \ \text{(template caps)}\end{aligned}$$

Walking through the model, decision by decision

Why shift templates, not per-hour headcounts? Because people work in contiguous blocks, not disconnected hours. The template set \(K\) encodes realistic shifts (lunch block, dinner block, split shifts), and \(a_{tk}\) records which hours each covers. Deciding \(y_k\) slots per template automatically produces hour-by-hour coverage that a human could actually staff. This "columns = feasible work patterns" idea is set covering's core, and the same trick as Example 2's batches.
Where the forecast enters — and why a quantile, not the mean. Required Dasher-hours are \(r_t = q_\alpha(\hat d_t)/\mu\): take the demand forecast's \(\alpha\)-th percentile, divide by productivity \(\mu\) (orders per Dasher-hour). Staffing to the mean would leave you short half the time. The right \(\alpha\) comes from newsvendor economics: if an unserved order costs \(c_u = \$12\) (lost margin + churn) and an idle Dasher-hour costs \(c_o = \$6\) in guarantees, staff to \(\alpha = 12/(12+6) = 0.67\) — the 67th percentile of demand. This one calculation connects your forecasting model directly to a staffing decision, and is the single most impressive thing to say in this example.
Why the show-rate \(\sigma_k\)? You don't command Dashers — you open slots and they self-select. If historically only 80% of claimed dinner slots produce an active Dasher, effective coverage is \(0.8\,y_k\). Ignoring this systematically understaffs. It also flags the deeper truth that "supply" here is influenced, not controlled — which is why incentives appear in the follow-ups.
Why the elastic shortfall \(s_t\)? Some hours may be impossible to cover (limited pool, unpopular time). The shortfall variable with cost \(v_t\) keeps the model feasible and — more usefully — prices each uncovered hour so you know exactly where coverage pain concentrates. Post-solve, sorted \(s_t\) values are your incentive-targeting list.
Integrality note. \(y_k\) are integers, but at counts of hundreds per template, solving the LP and rounding up costs pennies — say "I'd relax to LP at this scale" to show you know when integrality matters (it matters for 3 vans; not for 300 Dashers).

Follow-up questions & strong answers

What if opened slots go unfilled — supply just doesn't show up?
Then pay is the decision variable, not just slot count. Model show-rate as increasing in the incentive: \(\sigma_k(c_k)\) estimated from historical fill curves, made piecewise-linear so the optimization stays a MIP — now the model chooses both how many slots and at what pay level (a menu of (template, pay-tier) columns, one \(y\) per combination — the multiple-choice pattern). The dual view is elegant: the shadow price on the coverage constraint for hour \(t\) tells you the maximum bonus worth paying for one more Dasher-hour there — the model literally prices its own incentives.
How does this extend across zones — demand is spatial, and Dashers move?
Add a zone index: coverage constraints per (zone, hour), templates per zone. The interesting coupling is that Dashers can reposition mid-shift: model cross-zone flows \(f_{z\to z',t}\) with travel times as a small transportation/flow layer on top of covering. Practically I'd start with zones independent (clean, decomposable), quantify how often adjacent zones have opposite-sign shortfalls (the repositioning opportunity), and add the flow layer only if that's material — an evidence-driven complexity escalation, which is the tone interviewers reward.
Your demand forecast will be wrong. How wrong is too wrong, and how would you know?
The model already consumes uncertainty through the quantile \(q_\alpha\) — so the first-order question is whether the forecast's quantiles are calibrated: does actual demand exceed the p67 forecast about 33% of the time? That's a monitoring metric (pinball loss / coverage tests per zone-hour, §ML guide). Second, do a decision-sensitivity backtest: replay past weeks with the forecast vs. perfect hindsight and measure the staffing cost gap — if the gap is small, forecast accuracy isn't the binding problem. Third, robustness: if a zone's forecast has fat tails (new market), widen to a higher \(\alpha\) there specifically. Evaluating the forecast through the decision it feeds rather than by wMAPE alone is the senior answer.
Dashers dislike being told when to work. Does this model conflict with flexibility?
The model opens opportunities, it doesn't assign people — slots are offers into a self-selection market, which is exactly why \(\sigma_k\) and pay-responsive fill rates are in the model. If leadership wants stronger guarantees (e.g., committed schedules for a subset who opt in), model two supply tiers: committed slots (high \(\sigma\), higher cost) and open slots (lower \(\sigma\), cheaper), and let the optimization find the mix. Framing a labor-policy question back as a portfolio-of-supply-types optimization keeps you on technical ground while acknowledging the human constraint.
Example 4 — Incentive budget allocation across zones (knapsack/LP with diminishing returns)

Prompt. "You have a \$B weekly incentive budget. Allocate it across zone-hours to maximize incremental deliveries."

Clarify: uplift estimates \(g_{zh}(b)\) = incremental deliveries in zone-hour \((z,h)\) per budget \(b\) (from causal models — say the coefficients come from uplift modeling, not raw correlations); diminishing returns; possibly minimum-coverage fairness across zones.

Decisions

\(b_{zh} \ge 0\): budget for zone-hour \((z,h)\). Approximate each concave \(g_{zh}\) by \(P\) linear pieces with slopes \(m_{zh1} > m_{zh2} > \cdots\) and piece caps \(w_{zhp}\).

Model (LP — no binaries needed: concave maximization)
$$\begin{aligned}\max\ & \sum_{z,h}\sum_p m_{zhp}\, b_{zhp}\\ \text{s.t. } & \sum_{z,h,p} b_{zhp} \le B; \qquad 0 \le b_{zhp} \le w_{zhp}\\ & \sum_{h,p} m_{zhp} b_{zhp} \ge \underline{G}_z \ \ \forall z \ \ \text{(optional fairness floor)}\end{aligned}$$

Walking through the model, decision by decision

Get the coefficients right before the math. The uplift curves \(g_{zh}(b)\) must be causal — "incremental deliveries caused by spending \(b\) here" — not correlational. Historical spend correlates with demand spikes (you spent where things were busy), so a naive regression wildly overstates returns. Say explicitly: coefficients come from uplift modeling / past incentive experiments, and this framing alone separates you from candidates who optimize garbage inputs beautifully.
Why diminishing returns, and what it buys. The first \$100 in a zone-hour mobilizes the most price-sensitive Dashers; the next \$100 works on less responsive ones. So \(g_{zh}\) is concave. The payoff: a concave function being maximized linearizes with no binaries — approximate it by segments with decreasing slopes \(m_{zh1} > m_{zh2} > \cdots\), give each segment its own bounded variable, and the LP automatically fills steeper segments first (it would never skip a better slope for a worse one). No SOS2, no integer machinery — recognizing when convexity does the work for you is exactly the §5 skill.
The budget constraint is the whole game. One coupling constraint \(\sum b_{zhp} \le B\) ties thousands of otherwise-independent zone-hours together. At the optimum, its dual \(\lambda^*\) is the marginal value of budget: every funded segment has slope \(\ge \lambda^*\), every unfunded one \(< \lambda^*\). The optimal policy is literally "fund everything whose marginal uplift-per-dollar clears the bar \(\lambda^*\)."
Fairness floors change the story just enough. A minimum uplift \(\underline G_z\) per zone forces spending into zones that don't clear the global bar. The dual on that floor tells you exactly what the fairness policy costs in foregone deliveries — a number worth handing to the business.
Sanity checks. Units: \(m\) in deliveries per dollar, \(b\) in dollars. Degenerate case: if all curves were linear (no diminishing returns), the LP would dump the entire budget into the single best zone-hour — the concavity is what produces sensible diversified allocations. Scale: (zones × hours × pieces) ≈ 10⁴–10⁵ bounded variables, one coupling constraint — solves instantly.

Follow-up questions & strong answers

Marketing wants to add fixed-cost campaign choices — a zone either gets a promo campaign (with a creative/setup cost) or not. What changes?
Fixed costs break pure concavity, so binaries enter: \(z_{c} \in \{0,1\}\) per campaign with cost \(F_c\), and spend in that campaign only if selected — the fixed-charge pattern \(b \le M z_c\) with \(M\) = the campaign's spend cap (tight, per §5). The problem becomes a multiple-choice knapsack — still a very solvable MIP at realistic sizes. The structural sentence to say: "continuous spend with diminishing returns ⇒ LP; discrete campaign on/off decisions ⇒ MIP; the formulation absorbs both."
Your uplift estimates carry uncertainty — some zones have almost no experimental data. Spend there anyway?
Two complementary moves. Robustness: optimize on lower-confidence-bound slopes rather than point estimates, so poorly-estimated zones must clear a higher bar — protects the downside. Exploration: that alone starves data-poor zones forever, so reserve a small exploration budget (say 5%) allocated to high-uncertainty zone-hours specifically to buy information — a bandit-flavored explore/exploit split. Then close the loop: this week's spend produces next week's uplift estimates. Saying "the allocation is also an experiment design" shows systems thinking beyond the LP.
Incentives shift Dasher supply, which changes delivery times, which changes demand. Your uplift curves assume a static world. Problem?
Yes — it's a feedback loop, and the honest answer has three tiers. (1) At weekly cadence and moderate budget changes, treat curves as locally valid and re-estimate weekly — the standard production practice, fine for small moves. (2) For big-swing reallocations, the curves are off; run the new allocation as a regional experiment before full rollout, measuring realized uplift vs. predicted. (3) The full equilibrium treatment (supply responds to pay, demand responds to service quality) is a market-simulation exercise — worth it only for strategic questions like entering a new metro. Matching the modeling ambition to the decision's blast radius is the judgment being tested.
How would you know, after the fact, that this optimizer beat what the ops team was doing manually?
Holdout-based measurement: keep a set of comparable zone-weeks under the manual policy (or a stratified switchback between policies) and compare incremental deliveries per dollar — the same causal discipline demanded of the inputs. Also track the model's own predictions vs. realized uplift per zone-hour: a calibration check on the curves that doubles as drift monitoring. Guardrails: Dasher earnings distribution and per-zone service levels, so the optimizer isn't quietly winning the metric by abandoning hard zones — which the fairness floors exist to prevent.
Example 5 — DashMart / dark-store location (facility location)

Prompt. "Where should we open dark convenience stores in a metro, and which neighborhoods does each serve?"

Clarify: candidate sites with lease + ops fixed costs; neighborhood demand forecasts; delivery cost/time grows with distance; service radius promise (≤ 30 min); budget or store-count cap.

Sets/parameters

\(I\): candidate sites (\(f_i\) fixed cost, \(Q_i\) capacity); \(J\): demand cells (\(d_j\) forecast orders); \(c_{ij}\): cost to serve cell \(j\) from site \(i\) (∞ / excluded if time > 30 min).

Model
$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & \sum_i f_i z_i + \sum_{i,j} c_{ij}\, d_j\, x_{ij}\\ \text{s.t. } & \sum_i x_{ij} = 1 \ \forall j; \quad x_{ij} \le z_i \ \forall i,j; \quad \sum_j d_j x_{ij} \le Q_i z_i \ \forall i\\ & \sum_i z_i \le p; \qquad z_i \in \{0,1\},\ x_{ij} \ge 0\end{aligned}$$

Walking through the model, decision by decision

Two coupled decisions, two variable families. Strategic: open site \(i\) or not — binary \(z_i\), carries the fixed cost \(f_i\). Operational: which store serves which neighborhood — \(x_{ij}\), the fraction of cell \(j\)'s demand served from site \(i\). The objective adds fixed costs and demand-weighted service costs in the same (dollar) units. Most location problems decompose this way: expensive irreversible decisions + cheap adjustable ones.
The linking constraint, and why its form matters. \(x_{ij} \le z_i\) says "you can't serve from a closed store." The tempting aggregate version \(\sum_j x_{ij} \le n\,z_i\) is logically equivalent but has a far weaker LP relaxation (a fractional \(z_i = 0.02\) can "open" 2% of a store cheaply). The disaggregated per-(i,j) form makes the relaxation tight and the branch-and-bound tree small — this is the concrete instance of §7's "bigger but tighter beats smaller but weaker," and citing it here is a strong-signal moment.
Service radius as variable pruning. The ≤30-min promise isn't a constraint row — just don't create \(x_{ij}\) for pairs beyond the radius. Modeling by omission keeps the matrix small and is the same sparsification instinct as Example 1.
Capacity closes a loophole. \(\sum_j d_j x_{ij} \le Q_i z_i\) caps each store's assigned demand. Without it, the model funnels a whole metro through one cheap store. Note the units check: both sides in orders/week.
Why \(x\) can stay continuous. If split service is acceptable (a neighborhood served by two stores), \(x_{ij} \ge 0\) suffices and the model solves faster; if single-sourcing is a business rule, flip \(x\) to binary and note the solve gets harder. Asking the interviewer which one the business wants is a natural, gradeable clarifying question.
Scale reality. Tens of candidate sites × thousands of demand cells ⇒ a MIP with a few dozen binaries and ~10⁵ continuous variables — solves in seconds. Unlike routing, there is no reason to reach for heuristics here; say so.

Follow-up questions & strong answers

Demand forecasts for neighborhoods you've never served are shaky. How do you make a multi-year lease decision on them?
Two-stage stochastic programming, explained simply: locations are first-stage (decided now, shared across futures); assignments are second-stage (adjust per demand scenario). Build 20–100 demand scenarios (growth rates, cannibalization levels, competitor entry), replicate the assignment variables per scenario, keep one set of \(z_i\), and minimize fixed cost + expected service cost. The solution favors sites that are robust across futures rather than optimal for one forecast. If the scenario model gets big, mention Benders decomposition as the standard tool (§12) — locations in the master, per-scenario LPs generating cuts. Also offer the cheaper alternative: solve per scenario, and pick sites appearing in most solutions — a defensible heuristic when stakes don't justify the machinery.
Nearby stores cannibalize each other's demand. Your model treats demand cells as fixed. Isn't that wrong?
Partially — the model assigns fixed demand, it doesn't shrink it when a competitor-sibling opens nearby. Practical fixes in order of ambition: (1) build cannibalization into the demand inputs — a gravity/market-share model where cell demand splits by store attractiveness and distance; (2) add pairwise penalties or minimum-spacing constraints (\(z_i + z_{i'} \le 1\) for site pairs within 1 km — the at-most-one logic pattern); (3) iterate: solve, re-estimate demand under the chosen network, re-solve — a fixed-point loop that usually converges in a few rounds. The honest meta-point: location models are only as good as their demand model, so I'd stress-test the chosen network against pessimistic cannibalization assumptions before signing leases.
Real estate isn't a clean menu — sites become available at different times, leases have terms. Does the one-shot model survive contact with reality?
The one-shot model is the right evaluation engine, wrapped in a rolling process: re-run it quarterly with the current network fixed (\(z_i = 1\) for existing stores, with closure variables if exits are allowed), the newly available sites as candidates, and multi-period demand scenarios. For explicit timing decisions, extend to a multi-period MIP — open/close variables per period with lease-term constraints — which stays very solvable at tens of sites. The habit being displayed: strategic models run repeatedly inside a decision process, not once.
The 30-minute service promise — hard constraint or something softer?
Interrogate it before encoding it: is 30 minutes contractual, or a target? If hard, it's variable omission (no \(x_{ij}\) beyond radius) — clean but brittle: a cell reachable only at 32 minutes becomes unservable. If it's a target, keep those pairs with a steep service-time penalty in \(c_{ij}\) — coverage degrades gracefully and the solution tells you exactly which cells are expensive to promise. I'd present both and recommend soft-with-steep-penalty, because real coverage maps have awkward edges and the business usually prefers 'served slightly slow' to 'not served'. This is the §6 hard-vs-soft taxonomy applied live.
Example 6 — Multi-pickup route with promise times (small VRPTW)

Prompt. "A Dasher holds a batch of 3 orders: 2 pickups and 3 dropoffs. Sequence the stops to minimize total lateness against each order's promised time."

Clarify: precedence (pickup before its dropoff); travel times \(\tau_{uv}\) given; food-ready times \(e_u\) at pickups (can't leave earlier); promises \(d_v\) at dropoffs; lateness is soft (penalized), route duration also matters.

Sets/decisions

Nodes \(V\) = {start, pickups, dropoffs}; \(x_{uv}\in\{0,1\}\): arc \(u\to v\) traversed; \(t_v \ge 0\): arrival time; \(L_v \ge 0\): lateness at dropoff \(v\).

Model
$$\begin{aligned}\min\ & \sum_{v \in \text{drops}} w_v L_v \;+\; \lambda \sum_{u,v} \tau_{uv} x_{uv}\\ \text{s.t. } & \text{degree: each node entered/left exactly once (path from start)}\\ & t_v \ge t_u + s_u + \tau_{uv} - M(1 - x_{uv}) && \forall u,v \ \ \text{(time propagation)}\\ & t_{p} \ge e_{p} \ \forall p \in \text{pickups}; \qquad t_{\text{drop}(p)} \ge t_{p} && \text{(ready time; precedence)}\\ & L_v \ge t_v - d_v,\; L_v \ge 0 && \forall v \in \text{drops (tardiness slack)}\end{aligned}$$

Walking through the model, decision by decision

Two variable families: sequence and time. Routing needs both what order (arc binaries \(x_{uv}\): "go from stop \(u\) directly to stop \(v\)") and when (continuous arrival times \(t_v\)). Keeping them separate and linking them with one constraint family is the clean architecture; models that try to encode timing inside sequencing variables get ugly fast.
The time-propagation constraint is the heart. \(t_v \ge t_u + s_u + \tau_{uv} - M(1 - x_{uv})\): if the route goes \(u \to v\) (\(x_{uv}=1\), big-M term vanishes), then arrival at \(v\) is at least arrival at \(u\) plus service plus travel. If not taken (\(x_{uv}=0\)), \(M\) deactivates the constraint entirely. It's the §5 indicator pattern doing scheduling work. Tight \(M\): the horizon length (say 90 min), never 10⁶.
Free subtour elimination — say this, it's the elegant bit. Classic TSP formulations need exponentially many constraints to forbid disconnected mini-loops. Here they're unnecessary: around any cycle, the time-propagation inequalities would force \(t\) to strictly increase back into itself — impossible. Time's arrow kills subtours for free.
Business rules land as simple bounds. Food-ready: \(t_p \ge e_p\) (arriving early at a pickup means waiting — if wait matters, add a wait variable and penalize it). Precedence: \(t_{\text{drop}(p)} \ge t_p\) — a dropoff can't precede its pickup. Both are one-liners once the time variables exist — the payoff of the two-family design.
Lateness as priced slack. \(L_v \ge t_v - d_v,\ L_v \ge 0\) — the tardiness pattern from §6/§13. The objective trades weighted lateness against route duration via \(\lambda\); weights \(w_v\) encode VIPs or already-re-promised orders. Lateness is soft because it must be: sometimes every sequence is late somewhere, and the model's job is choosing who eats the delay at minimum total damage.
The pragmatic punchline — deliver it explicitly. With ≤6 stops and precedence pruning, there are at most a few dozen valid orderings: brute-force enumeration solves this in microseconds. Present the MIP to demonstrate modeling command, then say enumeration is the production answer at this size — the MIP form matters when routing couples with upstream batching at scale. Knowing when the problem is small enough to enumerate is precisely the rigor-plus-pragmatism balance the rubric names.

Follow-up questions & strong answers

Travel and prep times are stochastic. Your times are point estimates — how wrong does this go, and what's the fix?
The failure mode: a sequence that's optimal on means but fragile — one slow kitchen cascades lateness down every later stop. Cheap fixes first: use padded or quantile travel/prep times (p70 rather than mean) so the plan carries slack where it hurts; re-optimize the remaining stops after every completed stop (trivial at this size — it's the rolling horizon again, at route scale). The heavier tool — stochastic routing over scenario samples — is rarely justified for 6 stops when re-sequencing en route is this cheap. Bonus point: evaluate sequencing policies in simulation on p90 lateness, not expected lateness, because the promise is a tail commitment.
Scale this up: 50 stops, multiple Dashers, live traffic. Now what?
Now it's real VRPTW and exact MIP is out. The production stack: construction heuristic (regret insertion) for an initial solution, improved by Large Neighborhood Search — repeatedly destroy ~20% of the route assignments and optimally repair — which is state of the art and what OR-Tools' routing solver implements; column generation / branch-and-price if provable quality is required offline. Two anchors worth stating: keep an exact solver on small instances as the quality benchmark, and note that in food delivery the effective routing instances stay small by design — batches of 2–4 — so the hard combinatorics lives in batching (Example 2), not in sequencing.
Why not just let the Dasher decide the stop order?
Partly you should — the Dasher sees ground truth (parking, building access) the model doesn't. The strong answer is a division of labor: the optimizer commits to the batch composition and a recommended sequence with predicted times (which feed customer ETAs); the app allows re-ordering, and deviations get logged. Then close the loop: if Dashers systematically reorder certain patterns, your travel/service-time model is biased — deviation rate becomes a model-health metric feeding retraining. Turning an autonomy question into a feedback-loop design shows product sense layered on OR.
Your objective mixes lateness and route duration with a weight λ. How do you set it?
Dollarize both sides: a Dasher-minute costs roughly the effective pay rate; a late-minute costs churn-adjusted lifetime value damage, estimable from historical cancellation/rating data as a function of lateness. If the business can't commit to a single rate, sweep λ to trace the Pareto frontier of (route time, p90 lateness) and present 3 operating points — the ε-constraint alternative from §6. Never leave a multi-objective weight looking arbitrary; show it's either priced from data or explicitly chosen by the business.
Practice protocol
  1. Re-derive each example from just its prompt line, by hand, without peeking. Target: complete sets → variables → objective → constraints in ≤ 10 minutes each.
  2. For each, prepare a 30-second "extension menu": uncertainty, fairness, scale, multi-objective — you'll be asked at least one.
  3. Rehearse your 2–3 past projects in the same 8-step language (§10): even storytelling should reveal sets/variables/objective/constraints, business impact numbers, and one tradeoff you navigated.

Heuristics worth naming (with one-line descriptions)

  • Greedy + regret: insert the item whose second-best option is worst first (regret insertion — much better than plain greedy for routing).
  • Local search / 2-opt / swap: improve by small exchanges until no improving move.
  • Large Neighborhood Search (LNS): repeatedly destroy (remove ~20% of assignments) and repair (re-insert optimally) — state of the art for VRP.
  • LP rounding + repair: solve relaxation, round confident variables, fix violations — a principled MIP warm-start too.
  • Simulated annealing / tabu: escape local optima; mention, don't dwell.
The sentence that wins this section"I'd start exact — a MIP with a gap tolerance — because it gives a provable bound; if latency or scale kills it, I keep the MIP as the offline benchmark and deploy a heuristic whose quality I can measure against that bound." Heuristic with a bound beats heuristic alone.
Decomposition in three intuitions (name-drop safely)
  • Column generation: master problem selects from a small pool of candidate "plans" (routes/batches/shifts); a pricing subproblem uses the master's dual prices to manufacture new plans with negative reduced cost. Stops when no improving plan exists ⇒ LP-optimal over the exponential set.
  • Lagrangian relaxation: move coupling constraints into the objective with price multipliers; the remainder decomposes (e.g., per-Dasher subproblems); adjust prices by subgradient. Gives bounds + good heuristic solutions.
  • Benders: fix the "hard" first-stage variables (e.g., facility openings); the remaining LP's duals generate cuts that teach the master about the second stage. The go-to for two-stage stochastic designs (Example 5 with scenarios).

You will rarely be asked to execute these live — but correctly matching the technique to the structure ("batching over an exponential set of candidate batches ⇒ column generation") is a strong senior signal.

ML ↔ OR: the boundary questions

As an MLE interviewing on OR, expect at least one boundary question. Crisp positions:

  • ML predicts, OR decides. ML turns raw data into coefficients (\(\hat t_{ij}\), demand quantiles, acceptance probabilities); OR turns coefficients + constraints into globally consistent decisions. Don't use ML to imitate the optimizer's output; use it to feed the optimizer better inputs.
  • Prediction error propagates. A biased prep-time model makes batching systematically too aggressive; discuss calibration and using quantiles, not means, where costs are asymmetric (§8 newsvendor).
  • When RL/heuristics-from-ML: sequential problems where the value of an action depends on stochastic futures (delay decisions, repositioning). But note rolling-horizon MIP re-solves are a very strong, more debuggable baseline — say you'd need evidence RL beats it in simulation before deploying.
  • Decision-aware ML (advanced flourish): training the prediction model against downstream decision loss ("smart predict-then-optimize") rather than pure MSE.
